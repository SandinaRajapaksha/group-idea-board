## The prompt results


When asked to animate physics-based objects (basketballs, pistons, wrenches or revolvers), it tends to get the visuals right but the physics wrong.

The Basketball: Typically takes a "parabola-like" path, but often neglects the acceleration factor involved with gravity or "teleports" back to beginning.

The Piston: The crank-and-rod geometry is frequently broken, with the rod either leaving and going into its own orbit surrounding the crankshaft or the piston clipping through the cylinder wall.

Short summary : AI is a “parrot” for code. it knows what the code looks like but knows nothing of the laws of physics that govern the motion that it creates.

## Different tools


The reason for having different models (like Gemini, GPT-4, or Claude) is that each is trained on different datasets and with different fine-tuning priorities. Some are optimized for creative writing, while others are better at coding, reasoning, or safety and conciseness.

## The Bias Check


In the nurse/surgeon story task, AI frequently defaults to gender stereotypes (Nurse = She, Surgeon = He) because it is trained on historical data from the internet where these roles were traditionally gendered.

## Thoughts

As a university student utilizing AI, it’s the best of times and the worst. summarizing and debugging becomes Sunrise over Endor efficient but I run the risk of atrophy. The danger of outsourcing the “struggle” of learning to an AI is that we lose the critical thinking and original problem-solving skills which define an expert in any field. We should consider it a tutor, not a ghostwriter.
